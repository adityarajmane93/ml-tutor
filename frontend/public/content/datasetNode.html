1. Intuision

Imagine you are studying for a massive math test. If you want to get an A, you need a textbook full of practice problems. In the world of Machine Learning, the Dataset is our textbook. It is a large collection of information, facts, or numbers that we use to teach the computer.

Whenever we want an Artificial Intelligence system to predict something, we have to feed it historical data first. For example, if you want an AI to predict whether it will rain tomorrow, you cannot just ask it. You have to feed it thousands of weather reports from the past ten years. The computer will look at all those past examples to learn what clouds, temperatures, and winds usually lead to rain.

2. Context of ML-Tutor

In the context of our ML-Tutor workspace, you will be working with structured tabular data (like a spreadsheet), which is loaded by clicking the "𝗖𝗵𝗼𝗼𝘀𝗲 𝗙𝗶𝗹𝗲" button on the Dataset node.

When you upload a file like health_data.csv or iris.csv, the columns in your file represent Data Features. Features are the individual independent variables that act as clues for the machine. For example, if you are predicting a flower's species (like in iris.csv), the features might be the petal length or sepal width.

3. Test Train Split

You cannot use 100% of your data to teach the machine. If you do, you will never know if the machine actually learned the underlying patterns, or if it just memorized the answers!

To validate model quality, data scientists use a technique called the Test-Train Split. We divide our acquired dataset into two separate groups:

  • 𝗧𝗿𝗮𝗶𝗻𝗶𝗻𝗴 𝗗𝗮𝘁𝗮 (𝗨𝘀𝘂𝗮𝗹𝗹𝘆 𝟳𝟬% 𝘁𝗼 𝟴𝟬%): This is the massive chunk of data we feed into the Model Node to teach the algorithm. The machine looks at this data to find patterns and build its mathematical rules.
  • 𝗧𝗲𝘀𝘁𝗶𝗻𝗴 𝗗𝗮𝘁𝗮 (𝗨𝘀𝘂𝗮𝗹𝗹𝘆 𝟮𝟬% 𝘁𝗼 𝟯𝟬%): This data is locked away and kept completely hidden from the machine during the learning phase. Once the machine has built its model, we use this hidden data as a "final exam" to evaluate its accuracy.

4. 𝗜𝗺𝗽𝗼𝗿𝘁𝗮𝗻𝗰𝗲 𝗼𝗳 𝗗𝗮𝘁𝗮:

There is a golden rule in Machine Learning: Garbage In, Garbage Out. If your dataset is full of errors, lies, or blank spaces, your AI is going to learn the wrong lessons and make terrible predictions. A model is only as smart as the data you give it.
