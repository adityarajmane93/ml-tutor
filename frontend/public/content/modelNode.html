How Do Computers Actually Make Decisions?

There are two main ways to get a computer to make a decision:

  • 𝗔𝗽𝗽𝗿𝗼𝗮𝗰𝗵 𝗔: 𝗥𝘂𝗹𝗲-𝗕𝗮𝘀𝗲𝗱 𝗔𝗜 (𝗧𝗵𝗲 𝗦𝘁𝗿𝗶𝗰𝘁 𝗣𝗮𝗿𝗲𝗻𝘁): In a rule-based approach, the human programmer writes exactly what the computer should do. 𝘌𝘹𝘢𝘮𝘱𝘭𝘦: "𝘐𝘧 𝘵𝘩𝘦 𝘵𝘦𝘮𝘱𝘦𝘳𝘢𝘵𝘶𝘳𝘦 𝘪𝘴 𝘰𝘷𝘦𝘳 30 𝘥𝘦𝘨𝘳𝘦𝘦𝘴, 𝘵𝘶𝘳𝘯 𝘰𝘯 𝘵𝘩𝘦 𝘧𝘢𝘯." The computer doesn't learn anything; it just follows your strict rules.
  • 𝗔𝗽𝗽𝗿𝗼𝗮𝗰𝗵 𝗕: 𝗟𝗲𝗮𝗿𝗻𝗶𝗻𝗴-𝗕𝗮𝘀𝗲𝗱 𝗔𝗜 (𝗠𝗮𝗰𝗵𝗶𝗻𝗲 𝗟𝗲𝗮𝗿𝗻𝗶𝗻𝗴): Instead of giving the machine the rules, you give the machine the 𝗱𝗮𝘁𝗮 and the 𝗮𝗻𝘀𝘄𝗲𝗿𝘀, and it calculates the rules on its own! This is what your ML-Tutor app is doing.

How Does the Machine Learn?

When using Machine Learning, algorithms generally learn in a few different ways:

  • 𝗦𝘂𝗽𝗲𝗿𝘃𝗶𝘀𝗲𝗱 𝗟𝗲𝗮𝗿𝗻𝗶𝗻𝗴 (ML-Tutor uses this!!): This is like a teacher giving you flashcards with the answers on the back. The machine learns by looking at data that is already labeled.
  • 𝗨𝗻𝘀𝘂𝗽𝗲𝗿𝘃𝗶𝘀𝗲𝗱 𝗟𝗲𝗮𝗿𝗻𝗶𝗻𝗴: The machine is given a huge pile of data with no labels and has to find hidden patterns and group things together all by itself.

2. Context of ML-Tutor

If you look at the red-colored Model block in the app, you will see a drop-down menu called Algorithm. An algorithm is simply a step-by-step mathematical recipe that the computer follows.

ML-Tutor deals with "𝗖𝗹𝗮𝘀𝘀𝗶𝗳𝗶𝗰𝗮𝘁𝗶𝗼𝗻 𝗔𝗹𝗴𝗼𝗿𝗶𝘁𝗵𝗺𝘀" i.e. The AI would arrange things into groups based on learned patterns. 𝘌𝘹𝘢𝘮𝘱𝘭𝘦: 𝘐𝘴 𝘵𝘩𝘪𝘴 𝘢𝘯 𝘪𝘮𝘢𝘨𝘦 𝘰𝘧 𝘢 𝘥𝘰𝘨 𝘰𝘳 𝘢 𝘤𝘢𝘵? 𝘐𝘴 𝘵𝘩𝘪𝘴 𝘧𝘭𝘰𝘸𝘦𝘳 𝘢𝘯 𝘪𝘳𝘪𝘴 𝘰𝘳 𝘯𝘰𝘵?

ML models/Algorithms that ML-Tutor uses are:

  • 𝗞-𝗡𝗲𝗮𝗿𝗲𝘀𝘁 𝗡𝗲𝗶𝗴𝗵𝗯𝗼𝘂𝗿𝘀 (𝗞𝗡𝗡):
    • 𝗧𝗵𝗲 𝗔𝗻𝗮𝗹𝗼𝗴𝘆: "Tell me who your friends are, and I'll tell you who you are."
    • 𝗛𝗼𝘄 𝗶𝘁 𝘄𝗼𝗿𝗸𝘀:This algorithm looks at data points plotted on a graph. When a new, unknown data point arrives, the computer looks at its "𝗞" 𝗰𝗹𝗼𝘀𝗲𝘀𝘁 𝗻𝗲𝗶𝗴𝗵𝗯𝗼𝗿𝘀 (where 𝙆 is just a number, like 𝟱). The new data point adopts the category that the majority of its neighbors belong to.
  • 𝗟𝗼𝗴𝗶𝘀𝘁𝗶𝗰 𝗥𝗲𝗴𝗿𝗲𝘀𝘀𝗶𝗼𝗻:
    • 𝗧𝗵𝗲 𝗔𝗻𝗮𝗹𝗼𝗴𝘆: "Flipping a coin based on odds."
    • 𝗛𝗼𝘄 𝗶𝘁 𝘄𝗼𝗿𝗸𝘀:It uses a special mathematical formula to calculate the 𝗽𝗿𝗼𝗯𝗮𝗯𝗶𝗹𝗶𝘁𝘆 (a score between 𝟬 and 𝟭) that a data point belongs to a specific category. If the score is higher than a certain cutoff (like 𝟬.𝟱), it gets one label; if it's lower, it gets the other.
  • 𝗗𝗲𝗰𝗶𝘀𝗶𝗼𝗻 𝗧𝗿𝗲𝗲𝘀:
    • 𝗧𝗵𝗲 𝗔𝗻𝗮𝗹𝗼𝗴𝘆: "Dumb Charades : Guess the movie name by answering a series of small questions."
    • 𝗛𝗼𝘄 𝗶𝘁 𝘄𝗼𝗿𝗸𝘀: The computer creates a branching set of true/false questions based on the data's features. It asks the most important question first, and based on the answer, splits the data down different branches until it reaches a final prediction.

3. Key Takeaway

Generalization is the goal: Models must perform well on new, unseen data, not just the training dataset.
